## 1. Gradient descent: the core idea

**Definition.** To minimize a loss `L(θ)`, repeatedly step in the direction of steepest descent — the negative gradient:

`θ ← θ − η ∇L(θ)`

where η is the **learning rate**. The gradient `∇L` is the vector of partial derivatives (a *0.3* vector of *0.5* chain-rule terms).

**Intuition.** Imagine standing on a foggy hillside wanting the valley. You feel the slope under your feet and step downhill. Step size (η) too large → you overshoot and bounce; too small → you crawl. The gradient always points *uphill*, so we subtract it.

**Variants by batch size.**

- **Batch GD** — gradient over the *entire* dataset each step. Accurate, smooth, but slow and memory-hungry.
- **Stochastic GD (SGD)** — one example at a time. Noisy but fast; noise can help escape shallow minima.
- **Mini-batch GD** — a small batch (32–8192). The universal default: GPU-efficient and a good noise/stability balance. LLMs use huge effective batches (millions of tokens) via gradient accumulation.

```python
import numpy as np
# Fit y = 3x + 2 with mini-batch SGD from scratch
rng = np.random.default_rng(0)
X = rng.uniform(-1, 1, (500, 1)); y = 3*X[:, 0] + 2 + 0.1*rng.standard_normal(500)
w, b, lr, bs = 0.0, 0.0, 0.1, 32
for epoch in range(200):
    idx = rng.permutation(500)
    for s in range(0, 500, bs):
        j = idx[s:s+bs]
        pred = w*X[j, 0] + b
        err = pred - y[j]
        gw = 2*np.mean(err*X[j, 0]); gb = 2*np.mean(err)   # gradients of MSE
        w -= lr*gw; b -= lr*gb
print(f"learned w={w:.3f} b={b:.3f}")   # ~3.0, ~2.0
```

## 2. Learning rate: schedules and warmup

**Definition.** The learning rate η is the single most important hyperparameter. A **schedule** varies it over training; **warmup** ramps η from ~0 up over the first few hundred/thousand steps before decaying.

**Intuition.** Early on, weights are random and gradients erratic — a big step can diverge (especially with Adam, whose variance estimates are unreliable at step 1). Warmup eases in. Later, **decay** (cosine or linear to near-zero) lets the model settle into a sharp minimum, like lowering the temperature in annealing. Cosine decay with linear warmup is the de-facto standard for training Transformers (*1.3*).

**Example.** Typical LLM recipe: linear warmup over 2,000 steps to peak η ≈ 3e-4, then cosine decay to 10% of peak over the full run.

## 3. Momentum, RMSProp, Adam, AdamW

**Momentum.** Accumulate a velocity `v ← βv + ∇L`, then `θ ← θ − ηv` (β ≈ 0.9). It dampens oscillation across steep ravines and accelerates along consistent directions — like a heavy ball rolling downhill.

**RMSProp.** Divide the step by a running root-mean-square of recent gradients, giving each parameter its own adaptive rate. Great for non-stationary/RNN objectives.

**Adam.** Combines both: tracks a momentum estimate (first moment `m`) and a squared-gradient estimate (second moment `v`), bias-corrects them, and updates `θ ← θ − η·m̂/(√v̂+ε)`. Robust default with little tuning.

**AdamW.** Adam with **decoupled weight decay** — applies L2 shrinkage directly to weights instead of folding it into the gradient. This fixes a subtle bug in Adam+L2 and is the standard optimizer for training and fine-tuning LLMs today (*1.3*, *1.4*).

**Intuition.** Plain SGD treats all directions equally; adaptive methods give flat directions bigger steps and steep directions smaller ones. AdamW additionally keeps regularization honest, improving generalization at LLM scale.

```python
import torch, torch.nn as nn
model = nn.Linear(10, 1)
opt = torch.optim.AdamW(model.parameters(), lr=3e-4, weight_decay=0.01, betas=(0.9, 0.999))
sched = torch.optim.lr_scheduler.CosineAnnealingLR(opt, T_max=1000)
x = torch.randn(64, 10); target = torch.randn(64, 1); loss_fn = nn.MSELoss()
for step in range(3):
    opt.zero_grad()
    loss = loss_fn(model(x), target)
    loss.backward()
    torch.nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_(model.parameters(), max_norm=1.0)   # gradient clipping
    opt.step(); sched.step()
    print(f"step {step} loss={loss.item():.3f} lr={sched.get_last_lr()[0]:.2e}")
```

## 4. Gradient clipping; vanishing and exploding gradients

**Definition.** In deep or recurrent nets, gradients multiply across layers via the chain rule (*0.5*). If factors are <1 they shrink toward zero (**vanishing** — early layers stop learning); if >1 they blow up (**exploding** — loss goes NaN). **Gradient clipping** caps the global gradient norm (e.g., to 1.0) to tame explosions.

**Intuition.** Backprop is a long product; long products are unstable. Vanishing gradients starve early layers of signal; exploding gradients throw the weights off a cliff. Clipping is a seatbelt; architectural fixes (residual connections, LayerNorm — both central to Transformers in *1.1*/*0.5*, and gating in LSTMs) are the seatbelt-plus-airbag.

**Example.** RNNs on long sequences classically suffer vanishing gradients; LSTMs' gates (*0.5*) and Transformers' attention + residuals largely solve it, which is *why* the field moved to Transformers.

## 5. Loss functions

The loss is the objective optimization minimizes. Pick it to match the task and the *0.2* likelihood being maximized.

**Regression.**

- **MSE (L2)** — `mean((ŷ−y)²)`. Penalizes large errors quadratically; corresponds to a Gaussian likelihood. Sensitive to outliers.
- **MAE (L1)** — `mean(|ŷ−y|)`. Linear penalty, outlier-robust; corresponds to a Laplace likelihood. Gradient is constant-magnitude.
- **Huber** — MSE near zero, MAE in the tails; best of both.

**Classification.**

- **Binary cross-entropy** — `−[y log p + (1−y) log(1−p)]`. The negative log-likelihood of a Bernoulli (*0.2*). Standard for two-class problems.
- **Categorical cross-entropy** — `−Σ yₖ log pₖ` over softmax outputs. The multi-class generalization — and **exactly the LLM training loss** in *1.3*: at each position the model predicts a distribution over the vocabulary and is scored by the cross-entropy against the true next token.
- **Hinge loss** — `max(0, 1 − y·ŷ)`. Used by SVMs; rewards a margin, not calibrated probabilities.

**Representation / metric learning.**

- **Contrastive loss** — pulls matching pairs together, pushes non-matching apart beyond a margin. Trains embeddings (link to *4.1*).
- **Triplet loss** — anchor, positive, negative: keep the positive closer than the negative by a margin. Used in face recognition and retrieval embeddings.
- **InfoNCE** — the modern contrastive workhorse behind CLIP and text-embedding models.

**Distributional.**

- **KL divergence** — `Σ p log(p/q)` — asymmetric "distance" between distributions. Used in variational methods (VAEs), knowledge distillation, and RLHF/PPO regularization that keeps a fine-tuned LLM close to its reference model (*1.3*).

```python
import torch.nn.functional as F, torch
logits = torch.tensor([[2.0, 0.5, -1.0]])       # 3-class logits
target = torch.tensor([0])
print("cross-entropy:", F.cross_entropy(logits, target).item())
p = torch.tensor([0.7]); y = torch.tensor([1.0])
print("BCE:", F.binary_cross_entropy(p, y).item())
```

### 5.1 When to use which

| Task | Loss | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Regression, outliers rare | MSE | Smooth, Gaussian-optimal |
| Regression, outliers present | MAE / Huber | Robust to tails |
| Binary classification | Binary cross-entropy | Bernoulli NLL, calibrated |
| Multi-class / LLM next-token | Categorical cross-entropy | Softmax NLL (see 1.3) |
| Max-margin classifier | Hinge | SVM margin objective |
| Embeddings / retrieval | Contrastive / Triplet / InfoNCE | Shapes similarity space (4.1) |
| Match distributions / distill / RLHF | KL divergence | Compares full distributions |

## 6. Real-world industry use cases

**Finance — calibrated risk scoring.** Default/fraud models train with **binary cross-entropy** because lenders need *calibrated probabilities* (a "12% default risk" must mean 12%), not just a decision. AdamW with weight decay and gradient clipping stabilizes training on noisy, imbalanced tabular data; learning-rate decay prevents the late-training oscillation that would destabilize a regulated scorecard. Huber loss is common in price/volatility forecasting where fat-tailed jumps would otherwise dominate MSE.

**E-commerce — recommendations and embeddings.** Retrieval and recommendation models train product/user **embeddings with contrastive/InfoNCE losses** so that co-purchased items sit close in vector space (feeding the search of *4.1*). Ranking uses cross-entropy or pairwise losses. Mini-batch SGD with Adam over massive interaction logs, plus cosine LR schedules, is standard; large batches with in-batch negatives make contrastive training efficient.

## 7. Common pitfalls

- **Learning rate wrong.** Too high → divergence/NaN; too low → glacial. Always sweep; use warmup for Transformers.
- **No warmup with Adam on Transformers.** Early instability can wreck the run.
- **Forgetting `optimizer.zero_grad()`** in PyTorch — gradients accumulate and corrupt updates.
- **Wrong loss for the task.** MSE on classification, or feeding softmax'd probabilities into `cross_entropy` (which expects logits).
- **Ignoring exploding gradients** — clip the norm; watch for NaNs.
- **Class imbalance in the loss.** Use class weights / focal loss (ties to *0.1*).
- **Over-relying on Adam's defaults.** AdamW + tuned weight decay usually generalizes better.
- **Confusing loss with metric.** You optimize the loss (differentiable) but *report* the metric (*0.1*, e.g., F1); they need not match.

## Wrap-Up

**Through-line.** Optimization turns the static math of *0.3* and the likelihood objectives of *0.2* into actual learning: compute a loss, backpropagate to get gradients (*0.5*), and let an optimizer step downhill. The specific recipe here — mini-batch data, AdamW, cosine schedule with warmup, gradient clipping, and categorical cross-entropy — is precisely the recipe that trains modern LLMs in *1.3*. Master the intuition of "which loss encodes which likelihood, and which optimizer stabilizes which landscape," and the training chapters become recognition rather than mystery.

### Interview Questions & Answers

1. **Q: Batch vs stochastic vs mini-batch GD?** A: Full-dataset (accurate, slow), single-example (noisy, fast), and small-chunk (the GPU-efficient default balancing both).
2. **Q: What does the learning rate control, and why warm it up?** A: Step size; warmup avoids early divergence when gradients/Adam variance estimates are unreliable.
3. **Q: How does momentum help?** A: It accumulates a velocity that dampens oscillation across ravines and accelerates consistent directions.
4. **Q: Adam vs AdamW?** A: AdamW decouples weight decay from the gradient update, fixing Adam+L2 and improving generalization; it is the LLM standard.
5. **Q: Vanishing vs exploding gradients — causes and fixes?** A: Chain-rule products <1 shrink, >1 blow up; fixes include clipping, residuals, normalization, LSTM gates, and better init.
6. **Q: Why cross-entropy for classification?** A: It is the negative log-likelihood of the categorical/Bernoulli model, giving well-behaved gradients and calibrated probabilities.
7. **Q: MSE vs MAE?** A: MSE penalizes large errors quadratically (outlier-sensitive, Gaussian); MAE is linear and robust (Laplace).
8. **Q: What is gradient clipping?** A: Rescaling the gradient when its norm exceeds a threshold to prevent destabilizing updates.
9. **Q: What is the LLM training loss?** A: Categorical cross-entropy over the vocabulary at each position — next-token negative log-likelihood (*1.3*).
10. **Q: When use contrastive/triplet loss?** A: To learn embeddings where similar items are close and dissimilar far (retrieval, faces, recommendations — *4.1*).
11. **Q: Where does KL divergence appear in LLMs?** A: In distillation and in RLHF/PPO as a penalty keeping the tuned policy near the reference model.
12. **Q: You optimize loss but stakeholders want F1 — reconcile.** A: Optimize a differentiable surrogate (cross-entropy), then tune the decision threshold to maximize the reported metric.

### Mini-glossary

- **Gradient** — vector of partial derivatives pointing uphill.
- **Learning rate (η)** — step-size multiplier.
- **Epoch** — one full pass over the training data.
- **Weight decay** — L2-style shrinkage of parameters (decoupled in AdamW).
- **KL divergence** — asymmetric measure of distributional difference.
