## 1.2.0 Where this fits (the bridge from 1.1)

At the bottom of every Transformer sits the **embedding matrix** `E ∈ ℝ^(V × d_model)` (section 1.1.1). To use it, we must convert text into **row indices** — integers in `[0, V)`. That conversion is **tokenization**, and its inverse (**detokenization**) turns generated token IDs back into readable text at the output head (1.1.7).

Tokenization sits *outside* the neural network — it is a deterministic, learned-but-frozen preprocessing step — yet it silently controls almost everything downstream:

- the **context window** you can use (1.1 said attention is O(n²) in *tokens*, not characters),
- the **cost and latency** of every API call (you are billed per token),
- **multilingual** and **code/number** quality,
- and even certain notorious model **failures** ("glitch tokens," bad arithmetic).

> **One-line thesis:** *The Transformer never sees text — it sees a sequence of integers produced by the tokenizer. Understand the tokenizer and half of an LLM's "weird" behavior stops being mysterious.*

## 1.2.1 What Tokenization Is — and the Granularity Spectrum

**Definition.** **Tokenization** is the process of splitting raw text into discrete units called **tokens**, then mapping each token to an integer **ID** via a fixed **vocabulary**. A **token** is simply an entry in that vocabulary — it may be a word, a piece of a word, a single character, or even a byte.

**The core design question: how big should a token be?** There is a fundamental trade-off along a **granularity spectrum**:

- **1.2.1.a Word-level tokenization.**
  - *Idea:* one token per word ("running" → `running`).
  - *Pros:* short sequences, human-intuitive.
  - *Cons:* **huge vocabulary** (hundreds of thousands of words + inflections), and it **breaks on unseen words** — the dreaded **OOV (out-of-vocabulary)** problem. "GPT-4o" or a new brand name becomes `<unk>` (unknown), destroying information.

- **1.2.1.b Character-level tokenization.**
  - *Idea:* one token per character ("cat" → `c`,`a`,`t`).
  - *Pros:* tiny vocabulary, **no OOV** ever.
  - *Cons:* **very long sequences** → because attention is O(n²) in sequence length (1.1.3), this is computationally brutal; the model must also relearn how characters compose into meaning.

- **1.2.1.c Subword tokenization (the winner).**
  - *Idea:* frequent words stay whole; rare words split into meaningful **sub-word** pieces.
  - *Result:* a bounded vocabulary (~30k–200k), **no true OOV**, and reasonable sequence lengths. This is what essentially every modern LLM uses.
  - *Example:* "tokenization" → `token` + `ization`; the rare "unhappiness" → `un` + `happiness` (or `un`,`happy`,`ness`), reusing pieces the model already understands.

**Intuition.** Subword tokenization is a *compression scheme*: give short codes (single tokens) to common strings and spell out rare ones from smaller parts. It balances the two failure modes at the ends of the spectrum.

**Interview angle.** "Why not just tokenize by words?" (vocab explosion + OOV). "Why not by characters?" (sequence length → O(n²) cost, weaker signal). "What problem does subword tokenization solve, and what's the trade-off it strikes?"

**Finance use cases.**
1. **Novel tickers & identifiers:** A word tokenizer would map an unseen ticker or CUSIP to `<unk>`; subword pieces preserve partial information so an extraction model can still reason about it.
2. **Rare regulatory jargon:** Terms like "collateralization" decompose into reusable subwords, so the model handles specialized vocabulary without a bloated word vocabulary.

**E-commerce use cases.**
1. **Endless new product names:** New SKUs and brands ("Zephyr-X200") appear constantly; subword tokenization avoids OOV and lets search/description models degrade gracefully.
2. **User-generated typos & slang:** Misspellings in reviews ("amaaazing") split into subwords instead of collapsing to unknown, preserving sentiment signal.

## 1.2.2 Subword Algorithms: BPE, WordPiece, and Unigram/SentencePiece

Subword tokenizers differ in **how they learn the vocabulary** from a training corpus. Three families dominate; know how each builds and applies its vocab.

### 1.2.2.a Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE) — GPT family, Llama, RoBERTa
- **Definition.** Start from the smallest units (characters or bytes). Repeatedly find the **most frequent adjacent pair** of tokens in the corpus and **merge** it into a new token. Repeat until reaching a target vocabulary size. The ordered list of merges is saved and re-applied at inference.
- **Training example (tiny):** corpus `low, lower, lowest`. Characters: `l o w …`. The pair `(l,o)` is frequent → merge to `lo`; then `(lo,w)` → `low`; and so on. Common substrings become single tokens.
- **Encoding at inference:** apply the learned merges greedily in order.

**Byte-level BPE (crucial modern variant — GPT-2/3/4, Llama).**
- Operates on **raw bytes (0–255)** rather than Unicode characters. Because *any* text — emoji, Chinese, code, symbols — is ultimately bytes, byte-level BPE can encode **anything with zero OOV**, and its base vocabulary is just 256 bytes.
- *Example:* an emoji "🚀" or "$NVDA" is always representable as some sequence of byte tokens.

### 1.2.2.b WordPiece — BERT, DistilBERT
- **Definition.** Similar to BPE, but instead of merging the *most frequent* pair, it merges the pair that most **increases the likelihood of the training data** (a probabilistic criterion). Continuation pieces are marked with `##`.
- **Example:** "playing" → `play`, `##ing`; "unaffordable" → `un`, `##afford`, `##able`. The `##` tells the detokenizer these attach to the previous piece.

### 1.2.2.c Unigram Language Model / SentencePiece — T5, ALBERT, many multilingual & Llama-adjacent models
- **Definition (Unigram).** Start with a **large** candidate vocabulary and **prune** it: iteratively remove tokens that least hurt a unigram language-model likelihood, until reaching the target size. At inference it picks the most probable segmentation (and can sample alternative segmentations — "subword regularization" — for robustness).
- **SentencePiece** is the *toolkit/framing* (often wrapping Unigram or BPE) that treats input as a **raw stream including spaces**, encoding a space as a visible meta-symbol `▁` (e.g., "▁New▁York"). This makes it **language-agnostic** (no reliance on whitespace splitting — vital for Chinese/Japanese/Thai) and **losslessly reversible**.

**Comparison table (interview-ready):**

| Property | BPE | WordPiece | Unigram / SentencePiece |
|---|---|---|---|
| Build strategy | Merge most frequent pair | Merge pair maximizing likelihood | Prune from large vocab by likelihood |
| Direction | Bottom-up (grow) | Bottom-up (grow) | Top-down (shrink) |
| Marker | (often byte-level) | `##` continuation | `▁` for space |
| Used by | GPT, Llama, RoBERTa | BERT | T5, ALBERT, multilingual |
| OOV | None (byte-level) | Falls back to chars | None |

**Interview angle.** "Explain how BPE builds its vocabulary." "BPE vs WordPiece — what's the different merge criterion?" "Why does byte-level BPE never produce OOV?" "What is SentencePiece and why is it good for multilingual text?" "What's subword regularization?"

**Finance use cases.**
1. **Multilingual filings/statements:** SentencePiece handles mixed-script financial documents (e.g., English + CJK) without whitespace assumptions, enabling one model across regions.
2. **Consistent numeric/symbol handling:** Byte-level BPE guarantees currency symbols and codes ("¥", "£", "ISIN") are always encodable in an extraction pipeline.

**E-commerce use cases.**
1. **Global catalog coverage:** SentencePiece tokenization lets one model serve listings and reviews across many languages for an international marketplace.
2. **Robustness to messy input:** BPE/subword handling keeps novel brand strings and emoji in reviews meaningful for sentiment and search.

## 1.2.3 The Tokenization Pipeline (what actually runs)

**Definition.** Modern tokenizers (e.g., HuggingFace `tokenizers`) run a **four-stage pipeline**, not a single split. Knowing the stages explains many quirks.

1. **1.2.3.a Normalization.** Clean/standardize text: Unicode normalization (NFC/NFKC), optional lowercasing, accent stripping, whitespace cleanup. *Example:* "Café" may be normalized so the accented character is represented consistently. (Aggressive normalization can lose information — a design choice.)
2. **1.2.3.b Pre-tokenization.** Split text into coarse chunks (usually on whitespace/punctuation) so the subword model works within sensible boundaries. Byte-level BPE uses a special pre-tokenizer that also encodes leading spaces (GPT-2's famous `Ġ` marker for a space-prefixed token).
3. **1.2.3.c Model (the subword algorithm).** Apply BPE / WordPiece / Unigram to each chunk to produce final subword tokens, then map them to IDs.
4. **1.2.3.d Post-processing.** Add **special tokens** and structure (e.g., `[CLS] … [SEP]`, `<s> … </s>`, or chat-template markers) and produce the model's inputs (`input_ids`, `attention_mask`, sometimes `token_type_ids`).

**Why the leading space matters.** In many tokenizers " token" (with a leading space) and "token" (no space) are **different tokens**. This is a real source of subtle bugs when you assemble prompts by string concatenation.

**Interview angle.** "Walk through the stages of a tokenizer pipeline." "Why can the same word map to different tokens depending on a leading space?" "What does the `attention_mask` do?" (marks real vs padding tokens — ties back to the padding mask in 1.1.3).

**Finance use cases.**
1. **Consistent parsing of formatted figures:** Normalization choices affect how "$1,234.56" or "(1,234)" (accounting negatives) are handled before extraction.
2. **Reliable prompt assembly for compliance bots:** Awareness of leading-space/special-token behavior prevents malformed prompts that degrade regulated outputs.

**E-commerce use cases.**
1. **Clean handling of specs:** Normalization standardizes units and symbols ("5″", "5 inch") before the model reasons over product specs.
2. **Correct chat formatting:** Proper post-processing/special tokens keep a shopping assistant's multi-turn context well-structured.

## 1.2.4 Vocabulary, Special Tokens, and Chat Templates

**Definition — vocabulary.** The **vocabulary** is the fixed, finite set of tokens a model knows, with a bijection to integer IDs. Sizes vary: ~30k (BERT), ~32k (Llama 2), ~100k–128k+ (GPT-4, Llama 3), even ~256k (some multilingual models). **Larger vocab → shorter sequences (cheaper) but a bigger embedding matrix** (more parameters and memory). This is a real architecture trade-off connecting back to 1.1.1's `V × d_model` table.

**Definition — special tokens.** Reserved vocabulary entries with **structural meaning**, not literal text:
- **Boundary/format:** `<s>`/`</s>` or `[CLS]`/`[SEP]` (start/separator/end), `<pad>` (padding to equalize batch lengths — see the padding mask in 1.1.3), `[MASK]` (masked-LM training in BERT), `<unk>` (unknown fallback).
- **Chat/role tokens:** instruction-tuned models add markers like `<|system|>`, `<|user|>`, `<|assistant|>`, `<|im_start|>`/`<|im_end|>`, `[INST]`/`[/INST]` to delimit conversation roles.

**Chat templates (highly practical).** Each instruct model was fine-tuned with an **exact** role-formatting scheme. Feeding it text in the wrong format **silently degrades quality**. In practice you call `tokenizer.apply_chat_template(messages, ...)` so the correct special tokens are inserted — a detail that separates people who've shipped from those who haven't. (You'll rely on this constantly in Phase 2 onward.)

**Example.** A chat model might receive, under the hood:
```
<|system|>You are a helpful assistant.<|end|>
<|user|>Summarize this filing.<|end|>
<|assistant|>
```
The model learned to continue right after the final `<|assistant|>` marker.

**Interview angle.** "What are special tokens and why do they exist?" "What happens if you don't use the model's chat template?" "How does vocabulary size trade off against sequence length and model size?" "What's the difference between `[MASK]` and `<pad>`?"

**Finance use cases.**
1. **Structured multi-turn advisory bots:** Correct role tokens keep system-level compliance instructions separated from user queries so guardrails hold.
2. **Vocab tuning for domain:** A finance-adapted tokenizer/vocab can encode frequent domain terms as single tokens, cutting sequence length and cost for high-volume document processing.

**E-commerce use cases.**
1. **Persistent assistant persona:** System-role tokens hold brand voice and policy across a long shopping conversation.
2. **Efficient large-catalog generation:** A vocabulary that captures common product terms as single tokens reduces cost when generating millions of descriptions.

## 1.2.5 Context Window — the Hard Limit Tokens Impose

**Definition.** The **context window** is the maximum number of tokens (prompt **input + generated output** combined) a model can process at once — e.g., 4k, 8k, 32k, 128k, 200k, or 1M depending on the model.

**Why it's bounded (connecting to 1.1).** Two forces:
1. **Compute/memory:** attention is O(n²) in tokens (1.1.3) and the **KV cache** grows with context (1.1.9) — so longer windows cost quadratically more compute and linearly more memory.
2. **Training/positional limits:** the model only learned position information up to some length (1.1.2); RoPE scaling / YaRN can extend this, but not for free.

**What happens at the boundary.** Exceeding the window forces **truncation** (dropping tokens — usually the oldest/middle) or **chunking**. This limitation is *the* motivation for **Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)** in Phase 4: since you can't fit everything, you retrieve only the most relevant chunks. It also motivates **conversation memory/summarization** in agents (Phase 5).

**"Lost in the middle" (worth knowing).** Even within the window, models often use information at the **beginning and end** better than the **middle** — so *where* you place key context matters, not just whether it fits.

**Interview angle.** "What limits the context window?" "What do you do when input exceeds it?" "Why does long context cost so much?" (O(n²) + KV cache). "What is the 'lost in the middle' effect?"

**Finance use cases.**
1. **Whole-report analysis is impossible in one shot:** A 300-page annual report far exceeds most windows, forcing retrieval/chunking for Q&A — a direct push toward RAG.
2. **Context budgeting under compliance:** Fitting policy text + transaction detail + question within the window requires deliberate token budgeting in a compliance assistant.

**E-commerce use cases.**
1. **Catalog can't fit:** You cannot stuff a product catalog into the window, so retrieval over product embeddings is required for a shopping assistant.
2. **Long support chats lose early turns:** Older turns fall out of the window, motivating summarization/memory for continuity.

## 1.2.6 Token Economics — Cost, Latency, and Efficiency

**Definition.** Because models process and are **billed per token**, tokenization directly drives **cost and latency**. Providers typically price **input tokens** and **output tokens** separately, with **output usually more expensive** (it's generated autoregressively, one step at a time — see 1.1.8).

**Rules of thumb (memorize for interviews & estimates):**
- English: **~1 token ≈ 4 characters ≈ ¾ of a word**; roughly **750 words ≈ 1,000 tokens**.
- **Code, numbers, and non-English text tokenize *less* efficiently** — more tokens per character → higher cost and slower responses. (A "token tax" on many languages and on JSON/code.)
- **Latency is dominated by output length**, because each output token is a separate forward pass; input is processed in a parallel "prefill." So capping `max_tokens` is a primary latency/cost lever.

**Cost-control levers (used throughout later phases):**
- Shorter, tighter prompts; **retrieval instead of stuffing** (Phase 4).
- **Prompt/response caching** for repeated boilerplate.
- **Model routing** — send easy subtasks to a small/cheap model, hard ones to a frontier model (Phase 9 capstone).
- Limit `max_tokens`, use stop sequences, stream to improve *perceived* latency.

**Worked example.** A 1,500-word document ≈ ~2,000 input tokens. If a summary is ~250 words ≈ ~330 output tokens, you can estimate cost as `2000 × input_price + 330 × output_price`. Multiply by volume (e.g., 1M documents) to see why token efficiency is a real P&L line.

**Interview angle.** "How would you estimate the cost of an LLM feature?" "Why is output more expensive than input?" "Give three ways to cut token cost in production." "Why is non-English text more expensive?"

**Finance use cases.**
1. **Cost at scale:** Summarizing millions of transactions/emails makes token efficiency a direct budget item; routing simple cases to a cheap model saves substantial spend.
2. **Latency SLAs on a trading desk:** Output length and model size are tuned to hit strict response-time targets for time-sensitive assistants.

**E-commerce use cases.**
1. **High-QPS storefront chatbot:** Minimizing tokens per turn keeps each interaction profitable at millions of sessions.
2. **Bulk description generation:** Budgeted in tokens; caching shared boilerplate and trimming prompts cuts total cost dramatically.

## 1.2.7 Failure Modes & Practical Pitfalls (what breaks in the real world)

Tokenization is the hidden cause behind several well-known LLM quirks. Interviewers love these because they reveal depth.

- **1.2.7.a Poor arithmetic / number handling.** Numbers split inconsistently ("1234" might be `12`,`34` or `1`,`234`), so digit-level structure is obscured — a key reason LLMs miscompute and why we offload math to **tools/calculators** (Phase 5). Newer tokenizers sometimes force single-digit tokenization to help.
- **1.2.7.b Code sensitivity.** Whitespace, indentation, and braces consume many tokens; tokenizer choices materially affect coding performance and cost.
- **1.2.7.c Multilingual "token tax."** The same sentence in a low-resource language can take 2–3× the tokens of English, raising cost/latency and effectively shrinking usable context for those users — a fairness and economics issue.
- **1.2.7.d Glitch / "unspeakable" tokens.** Rare vocab tokens (e.g., the infamous `SolidGoldMagikarp`) that appeared in vocabulary but almost never in training can trigger bizarre, unstable outputs.
- **1.2.7.e Leading-space & boundary bugs.** " word" vs "word" are different tokens (1.2.3); naive string concatenation in prompt building can subtly change behavior.
- **1.2.7.f Truncation surprises.** Silent truncation when exceeding the window (1.2.5) can drop the very instruction you cared about — always check token counts.

**Interview angle.** "Why are LLMs bad at arithmetic, and how do you fix it in production?" "Why might the same feature cost more for non-English users?" "What are glitch tokens?" "How would you debug a prompt that behaves differently after a tiny text change?"

**Finance use cases.**
1. **Never trust raw LLM math on money:** Because of number tokenization, financial calculations are routed to deterministic tools/code, with the LLM orchestrating — critical for correctness and auditability.
2. **Guarding against truncation in compliance:** Token-count checks ensure mandatory disclosures/instructions aren't silently dropped from long prompts.

**E-commerce use cases.**
1. **Accurate pricing/quantities:** Totals and discounts are computed by code, not the LLM's token-based arithmetic, preventing checkout errors.
2. **Fair multilingual experience:** Awareness of the token tax informs model/prompt choices so non-English shoppers aren't degraded by shrunken effective context.

# Wrap-Up: 1.2 Tokenization

## The through-line (how it connects — backward to 1.1, forward to 1.3+)
The Transformer (1.1) consumes **token IDs** that index its embedding table, so we need a way to turn text into integers. Word-level tokenization explodes the vocabulary and hits OOV; character-level makes sequences too long for O(n²) attention → **subword** tokenization (1.2.1) is the compromise, learned via **BPE / WordPiece / Unigram-SentencePiece** (1.2.2) through a **normalize → pre-tokenize → model → post-process** pipeline (1.2.3). The resulting **vocabulary and special tokens** (1.2.4) define chat formatting and, together with attention's cost, the **context window** (1.2.5) — which in turn drives **token economics** (1.2.6) and a family of real-world **failure modes** (1.2.7). *Because the context window is finite, we will need RAG (Phase 4); because tokenized numbers are unreliable, we will need tool use (Phase 5); because tokens cost money, we will need routing and caching (Phase 8–9).*

## Rapid-fire interview checklist
- Word vs character vs subword tokenization — trade-offs; what is OOV.
- How BPE builds and applies its vocabulary; byte-level BPE and why it has no OOV.
- BPE vs WordPiece (merge criterion) vs Unigram/SentencePiece (prune + `▁`).
- The four tokenizer pipeline stages; why a leading space changes tokens.
- Special tokens; chat templates and what breaks without them.
- Vocabulary size ↔ sequence length ↔ embedding-matrix size trade-off.
- What bounds the context window; truncation; "lost in the middle."
- Token→cost estimation; why output > input cost; why non-English costs more.
- Why LLM arithmetic fails and how to fix it; glitch tokens.

## Mini glossary
**Token** — an entry in the vocabulary (word piece, char, or byte).
**Vocabulary (V)** — fixed set of tokens ↔ integer IDs.
**OOV** — out-of-vocabulary; input the tokenizer can't represent (solved by subword/byte-level).
**BPE** — byte-pair encoding; merge most frequent pairs.
**WordPiece** — likelihood-based merges; `##` continuation.
**Unigram / SentencePiece** — prune-from-large / space-aware, language-agnostic tokenizer.
**Special tokens** — reserved structural tokens (`<s>`, `[SEP]`, `<pad>`, chat-role markers).
**Chat template** — the exact role-formatting an instruct model expects.
**Context window** — max input+output tokens processed at once.
**Token tax** — extra tokens (cost) for non-English/code/numbers.
